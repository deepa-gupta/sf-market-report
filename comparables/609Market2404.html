What's been happening in the market — Jan to Mar 2026
Condos are bouncing back — and doing it faster than expected

After a few slow years, the SF condo market turned a corner in late 2025 and has accelerated into 2026. The 3-month median condo price hit $1,137,500 in March — up over 8% from a year ago. That's a meaningful jump from the 1–2% gains we saw through most of 2024.

There are far fewer homes to choose from

Condo inventory across San Francisco is down 37% compared to this time last year — just 338 units available citywide. The oversupply that kept prices soft in 2023–24 has been absorbed. Buyers now have fewer alternatives, which matters for how you position your offer.

The AI boom is creating a new wave of luxury buyers

San Francisco is the epicenter of the artificial intelligence industry. Companies are expanding rapidly downtown, and well-compensated tech workers are translating stock market gains and signing bonuses into real estate. Luxury home sales in the metro hit a $6.4M median between August and October 2025 — up 5% from 2024. This is the buyer profile for a Ritz-Carlton penthouse near the Financial District.

Buyers are moving faster — 60% more properties going under contract

The rate at which listings are going under contract rose roughly 60% compared to this time last year. This doesn't mean you need to panic — but it does mean that well-priced properties in desirable buildings are not sitting forever the way they were in 2023.

Downtown luxury highrises are still lagging — but not for long

The market recovery has been strongest in residential neighborhoods like Cow Hollow and Pacific Heights. Downtown highrises are improving, but are still seeing longer days on market than the city overall. This means there's still negotiating room — but that window is closing.

How to think about price

Rather than fixating on a single number, think about this as a comfortable range. The data supports good value at every point between $2,090,000 and $2,280,000. Your goal is to land somewhere in that band — not to win by paying the least, but to secure a home that will look like a smart decision for years to come.

Where to start $2,090,000

Opening at 5% below ask is supported by the comps and reasonable given the seller's situation. It gives both sides room to negotiate and signals you're a serious buyer — not someone fishing for a bargain.

Your comfort zone $2,090,000 – $2,250,000

Any number in this range is a strong outcome. At $2,250,000 — the top of this zone — you're paying $1,207/sf for a Ritz-Carlton penthouse duplex with Bay views and 2 valet parking spaces. The luxury tier median is $1,500/sf. There is no bad deal within this range.

If the seller pushes above ask $2,250,000 – $2,280,000

A small premium above ask can happen — especially given how much the seller has already conceded from their 2007 purchase price. Even at $2,280,000 ($1,223/sf), you're still well below where this building's product type trades. It's worth considering if it's what closes the deal.

Where to pause and reassess Above $2,300,000

If the negotiation pushes north of $2,300,000, step back and evaluate what's driving it. The data gets thinner as a justification above this point. It's not necessarily a deal-breaker — but it warrants a conversation before proceeding.

What works in your favor
Your leverage

The home is vacant, which means the seller is paying $3,955/month in HOA fees with no income from the property. Every month it sits unsold costs them real money. After 40 days on the market, that urgency is building.

Your leverage

The seller originally paid $2,715,000 in 2007 and is already listing at a $515,000 loss. They can't argue this is a rising-market situation. That psychological anchor is gone.

Smart ask

Include a request for the seller to prepay 3 months of HOA dues ($11,865) as a credit at closing. This doesn't change the official sale price on record, but it puts real money back in your pocket. It's also an easy concession for the seller to make without feeling like they're lowering their price.

Flexible close

Offer the seller their preferred closing timeline — whether that's 30, 45, or 60 days. A fast, certain close from a prepared buyer is genuinely valuable to someone carrying a vacant property.

Do first

Before submitting an offer, request the full HOA financials and reserve fund study. You want to confirm the building is financially healthy with no upcoming special assessments — particularly for an 18-year-old building with comprehensive amenities.
